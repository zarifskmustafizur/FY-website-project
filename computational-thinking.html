Computational Thinking

What is Computational Thinking?

Computational thinking is the process of taking a problem and providing a solution by breaking down the main problem into sub-problems (decomposition) and presenting the solution in such a way that it will be easy for a human or machine to carry out.

Structure of the Computational Thinking Unit

  1. The content you will learn is split up into blocks
  2. Each block is 2 weeks
  3. You will have 2 lectures per block i.e 1 lecture per week
  4. You will have a coursework assignment that will be due when the block ends
  5. You will have to write up a self reflection about the coursework assignment for each block (approx. 300 words)
  6. Your coursework will be done in groups
  7. You will have 1 workshop per block, which will relate to your coursework assignment for that particular block

What will you learn in Computational Thinking?

  1. Describe an algorithm using pseudocode and flowcharts.
  2. What decomposition and generalisation is and how to apply them to your algorithms
  3. What graphs are and how and why they are useful
  4. The basics of the complexity of algorithms (time and space) and how to count the number of steps in your algorithms
  5. How to combine algorithms
  6. What is recursion and how it works

How will you be assessed?

  1. Written exam at the end of the semester: 50%
  2. Group coursework: 40%
  3. Self reflections: 10%

Are there any additional resources?

  1. You will have access to the Unit Handbook, which contains additional information on the content you will learn
  2. The UoM library is a great source of information not only for computational thinking, but for all your other modules that you will study in the foundation year and in your future degrees
